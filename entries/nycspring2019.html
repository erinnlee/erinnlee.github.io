to be charmingly human

written for my technology, religion, future class

read as: pdf

I love feelings - I love all sorts of feelings...good, bad, happy, sad, mad, confused, anything and everything! I think I’m someone who feels things quite deeply as well; when I’m feeling wonderful, I feel as though my heart could explode from how much love it’s filled with. Yet, I think sometimes, people forget that if the spectrum skews so drastically one way, it’s a chance it might be similar in the other direction. For my final project, I explored a personal struggle of mine: communicating effectively.

To preface, I’d argue that I’ve been incredibly blessed with how patient all my friends, acquaintances, and strangers have been to me through and through, and I truly do believe that this universe has been quite kind to me. I deeply believe in karma, both good and bad, and I am constantly asking myself two questions as I move about my day:

  1. understanding that energy is finite, what is my highest point of contribution in a given time?
  2. am I negatively impacting someone’s life by contributing in whatever way deemed by the first question?

If I am able to determine what my highest point of contribution is and it does not negatively impact someone else’s life, logically, it’s able to dictate my decision making. But, sometimes applying logic on feelings seems contradictory [i.e. love]. Feelings, communication, emotions...it feels like a series of complex problems insolvable with its stubborn, subtle nuances and almost an infinite amount of edge cases to consider. Is there even a right answer? Not sure, but it’s worth at least to try. Right?

To me, I think it makes sense to first think about where this “limitation” to communicate authentically comes from. It seems like I’m always running - physically, mentally, emotionally, maybe even spiritually. Sometimes I joke that I’m eternally late to life; running from meeting to meeting, class to class, always giving myself *just* not enough time to get to my next destination on time, always sneaking in slightly late or running out slightly early. I play this game of extremes with myself - shifting between no schedule to micromanaging my life down to the minute of when I sleep, shower, and eat. Moderation ​would​ be an ideal solution, but habits are hard to break, and new habits are hard to make. So, long story short, I haven’t figured it out yet.

When I left New York for the first time after starting school, I missed the city before I even left the city. Starry-eyed freshman Erinn deemed New York as the place to be. After all, everything and anything that I could ever possibly imagine and want for, New York would have it. I felt this strongly, and I proudly walked around saying that this place, this New York City, was the city I would stay in forever. Yet, after sophomore spring and being absolutely pounded by deadlines and responsibilities and expectations, I spent almost every day in the summer after sitting on the roof of my sublet looking at the New York skyline. And what I slowly realized was that, what I thought was the world to me, didn’t even scratch the surface of what’s out there. I started thinking, “this can’t be it”, and I convinced myself that I had to leave. So, when I left New York for London in the fall of 2018, I embraced the change of scenery. I saw it as a new adventure, an opportunity to remove myself from my current situation. But, if I’m not running towards something, then logically, it seems like I’m running away. Looking back now, this might have been one of those times.

If sophomore spring was the definition of micromanagement, I spent my junior fall doing the absolute opposite. I went straight from manically checking things off my “productive” to-do list to ditching the list and simply asking myself, “did you get up and make your bed today? Did you make sure to fill your body with energy and nutrients? Were you able to have at least one meaningful interaction with the world?” I stopped seeking to keep in touch with things that reminded me of stressful situations (i.e. Columbia, school, recruitment) and focused solely on things that brought me joy (i.e. traveling, journaling, wandering). This shift in mindset brought upon loads of self reflection, yet also, a barrelling of tests of friendships and unforeseen consequences. Previously, I had been so focused on my external interactions and composure, but while in London, I focused on the internal, me.

I’ll be upfront in saying this: I might have been the happiest I’ve been in a very long while I was there. Maybe it was because school and grades were pass/fail. Maybe it was because of environment I was in: living off campus and steps away from a train station that whisked me to airports. Maybe it was the expectations I had set for myself. Maybe it was the people. Maybe it’s because I felt like a person first, student second. Maybe it’s because I finally ​had the time​ made the time to realize and internalize what made me happy (and what didn’t). Maybe I wanted the change and wanted it so badly, I was ready to do most anything for it. But whatever the reason was, I admittedly isolated myself a lot more than I would have allowed myself back at school in New York. I slowed down the world around me and pushed my senses to take in more.

I’ve always been bad about remembering things. I have the worst short term and long term memory. Sometimes I’d go entire conversations without remembering people’s names. But when I was abroad, there was more of an urgency to remember. The first time I felt it was when I traveled to Paris alone - I spent multiple days alone wandering the streets, sitting by the Seine, having only solo meals while people watching and journaling. I wanted to remember it all for some reason. It was all the small details that made such a big impact on me. But it’s never that simple: I couldn’t merely say that I wanted to remember because it’s not like I could snap my fingers and all the memories could come rushing back to me. So, I tried to take pictures to create a foundation for me, and I started journaling almost religiously. It’s through living in the present that helped me realize that no amount of amount of technology will ever be able to replicate what I saw and experienced. But it’s not the same...

Commitment is a twofold problem for me. On one hand, maybe it’s the way I’ve grown up, but I always feel like I need to be loosely affiliated to most everything that could remotely be career/school related, so if and when new opportunities sprout up, I have the freedom to be spontaneous. On the other hand, in terms of people (and I hate to admit it), but I think that I’m always inherently afraid that they will leave. I remember in 8th grade, on the last day of classes, all my “friends” were going room to room passing out artwork; I remember asking if I could join them, and all of them averted their eyes except for one who told me that they, as a group, decided that they didn’t want to be friends anymore. Oof - 8th grade Erinn’s heart was broken! I left for boarding school without a real support network, and it was really tough. Something similar happened in high school once too that I never did find peace with (yet). A classmate made me question all the relationships I had after he accused me of being “the worst person anyone could be around.” And the one friend I would have never second-guessed her loyalty to me stared silently at her shoes. I know my classmate’s words aren’t true, as I’ve been surrounded by some of the most amazing people on campus and beyond, and I ought to not let other people’s negativity get to me. There are still some days though I wonder if these fallouts could have been avoided if only we had communicated more openly, continuously.

So, every time I throw myself into a completely new environment, it’s always one of the hardest things to me, but probably the best thing for me. I remember the first day I arrived in London, I called my mom with tears in my eyes - I told her it had all been a mistake, that I wanted to go back home, that I was wrong for wanting to leave, leaving what I had. We, as humans, hate change; frankly, I think we absolutely despise it. Yet, as soon as change happens, we adapt almost immediately. So, in this state of immediate adjustment (less because I wanted to, but more because I know I needed to), I found myself being more vulnerable with myself to the world. And because of this vulnerability, my heart was open to tuning in and finding others who were just as open and honest with their nervousness.

In this special space called London, I focused less on breadth, as I’ve done in New York, but rather on depth, something for some reason, I have been so scared to do. I don’t know why because I’m so happy I did - it’s not as scary as I thought it would be. Admittedly, the amount of people I met while I was in London were few, but they were and continue to be so dear to me even as we’ve returned to our original homes. They are the ones who have taken me for who I am, as I am, however I am. There are people like that here at school too. The friends I have now are people like that - I just have to open up. I have to trust.

Above all else though, I grew exponentially in a way I know I couldn’t have in that given time and space. What caused the growth? Frankly, it might have been from an internal urge to slow down, to reflect, to breathe...to be comfortable with being alone! I was surrounded by individuals who were constantly asking themselves deep and meaningful questions that helped to further extend their emotional intelligence. I stumbled upon this diagram a bit of time back, and I’ve kept it in my back pocket since then.

I took the time. We simultaneously have no time and all the time in the world - what’s the rush? Time only exists in relativity, and it only makes sense relative to what we prioritize. So, grappling with this new realization, there’s a side of me that wants to continue exploring that “surprise growth factor”. I think I’m going to be off soon again.

No one warned me about the displacement I would feel coming back to school. Four months...four months isn’t a long time in a long term sense. However, relative to the time we have in college, it’s 12.5% of my ​entire​ college experience. Woah. And because I wasn’t the best about keeping in touch with people, I found myself constantly assessing and reassessing all the interactions I had with others once I got back. Was it always like this? Is it weird if I reach out to her again? Am I bothering him? Why doesn’t anyone want to go on a walk with me? I thought incredible (almost embarrassing) amounts of hours thinking about when I was taking up more space in other’s hearts or when I gave more space than they might have wanted to have. For a while, I was obsessed with this irrational (yet seemingly logical) fear. I imagined that if I was in a room with everyone I loved and everyone who loved them, and someone in the room called out, “find partners!” and counted down...3...2...1...I’m not sure what I’m more nervous about: who would I look at? Who would look at me?

It’s taken an entire semester to internalize her words. Because half of me wants to believe it for its full worth, but the other half of me is afraid to. I’ve found myself running away from the very people who have been my biggest supporters whenever I got stressed and anxious (which was often). The hardest thing about coming back is understanding that I ​know​ I have the ability to be a person first, but also realizing that I either have to conform to the stress culture here to stay afloat or sink. Even now, I sit around friends who have pulled countless all nighters, who have picked at their skin and nails out of anxiety, who drink cups and cups of coffee...for and at what cost? As I’m inching towards the end of my time in college, I’m starting to think that there ought to be more options out there. I keep telling myself, “my intelligence is not based strictly on my academic performance; to me, I know walking away from college much more happier knowing that I made meaningful relationships with my peers, professors, and individuals both on campus and in New York than if I aced a final I didn’t sleep days for.” And I’m not a person who can have both. I can’t do it at all. I have to be okay with that. When we accept ourselves for who we are, as we are, however we are, each and every day, no matter how contradictory we can be to ourselves, that’s when we’re able to step into our greatest selves.

I am still working on myself. There are still lots of days where I feel like throwing in the towel. Sometimes I feel like a shipwreck happening live, white flag waving pathetically in the air. Other times, I feel like I’m in quicksand. I think that I am overly happy as more of a defense mechanism than not; if I didn’t try so hard, then I’m afraid I’d be a miserable mess. I wish I could somehow explain what it feels like to live with the anxiety and sadness I do, and how appearances aren’t everything. It’s the scariest feeling in the world waking up and having absolutely no ability to get out of bed. I wish I could explain the mind body separation where my body won’t let me get out of bed. But like the conversation in class, you can’t explain color to a blind person or drunkenness to someone who doesn’t drink - there’s nothing like experiencing it themselves. How do I logically explain that sometimes, I feel sad for no reason and there possibly is not anything that has happened to me, but all I can help is to feel this way anyway. Technology doesn’t help either. It’s a lot easier to lie to a screen than to someone’s face. Quick texts like, “I’m fine - I think I’m just tired” or “Sorry for cancelling today!! I don’t know why I couldn’t wake up,” are easy to send. I’m not lying either. I ​was​ okay at one point, and I ​am​ tired right now. I’ve given ​a truth, but maybe not ​the​ truth. But I always push myself to remember that there are days right after these sorrowful ones where I feel absolutely brilliant - that the world is on my side and happy accidents come in the most charmingly human ways.

How paradoxical our emotions are.

Since finishing this, I’ve found people who are willing to on walks with me and tell me about themselves. I’ve reconnected with old friends (like, NSOP three years ago kind of friends) and have chatted how both of us have grown. My emotions still come in waves, don’t get me wrong. But I’m more accepting of it now. Growing up, my mom always called me selfish because I was always more concerned about my well being. But I’m not sure how I could take care of anyone else if I couldn’t even take care of myself.

So, in the spirit of continuing to explore the “exponential surprise growth factor”, I’m leaving New York for a bit again - this time to Melbourne, Australia. I had countless qualms about if this was something I should pursue or not. To leave again doesn’t seem fair to me, to friends, to New York. But beginnings always hide themselves in endings. And when I go back to the two questions from the beginning of this journey, I do believe that Australia is the place where I’m able to give myself the highest value of contribution to myself (important to note!!), and I don’t think it negatively impacts anyone’s lives. I’m slightly sad to leave for another semester, but truthfully, mostly nervous and excited. My tummy does little somersaults when thinking about how often the sun will kiss my skin and how I might even see a kangaroo! I’m really interested to see how I grow, especially because now I have a grasp on what I am good at, what I am not so good at, and how I find peace and mindfulness in this universe. I can’t predict the future, but I do remember saying that I always think being conscious and mindful can’t live in the same space as technology in the beginning of the term. Throughout this semester (and through my conversations and walks and chats), I realize my growth happens when I share my experiences with people both in front of me and across screens. After all, I wouldn’t be convinced to have gone to Melbourne if it wasn’t for those I kept in touch through technology. Again, I can’t predict the future, but maybe I will come back slightly different; perhaps more mature. Perhaps kinder to myself. But this time around, I’m not running away.
